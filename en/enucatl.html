Enucatl

Divinity in the ancient aztec mythology. He is, in the tradition of many mesoamerican parchments, the brother of Quetzalcoatl, from which he differs by the lack of feathers; he is usually depicted as a snake with bat wings, a sharp bifurcate tail, a red mop, an elongated crocodile muzzle with deadly tusks.

He belongs to the group of chthonic deities, his main role is tauroboliaste and psychopomp: his most important task is indeed guiding the souls to the Mictlan, the underworld of precolombian religions. According to the legends, it was not rare that this deity, known for his noble soul, would be moved to pity by the dead, and allowed them to resurrect in their bodies: these events were often followed by gigantic earthquakes, and sometimes by powerful tsunamis. The research of the anthropologist John C. Gnostolds (1898-1973), near the sacred town of Sobbabia, uncovered many tombs with no bodies inside: a conclusive proof (as he theorized in his major work, The ancient cult of Enucatl — A scientific research on Mexican holy cities 1961-68 , published in 1970) of the mass resurrection.

The temple belt that runs from Sobbabia to Tenochtitlan is a proof of the devotion of the aztec people — as well as the mayan, according to some artefacts found in the Yucatan peninsula — for this deity: pyramids with rounded steps, 45 to 60 m high on average, on top of which human sacrifices would be performed to honor the god; with the decline of the aztec military power, and therefore the lack of slaves, the temples in the belt of the sacred towns were abandoned and fell into ruin. The cult of Enucatl also came to an end (see John C. Gnostolds, The ancient cult of Enucatl — A scientific research on Mexican holy cities 1968-1973 , published in 1980). We have to say that Azazale was not the last ruler of the Mexica dynasty to celebrate solemn ceremonies in the complex in Sobbabia; after his death (1321) the tyrant Maurus (1300-1322) succeeded him, but he was condemned to an almost complete damnatio memoriae for his extreme brutality; apparently, he gathered the army and quickly defeated the neighboring peoples, who had dared to revolt, and brought 10 000 prisoners to death in the honor of Enucatl.

The believers of the serpent-god resisted, mainly in the countryside, with the name of ajaxes; but the arrival of the Spanish in 1521 lead to the destruction of most of the documents on Enucatl and put an end to his remembrance. Only in the '60s the research of John C. Gnostolds restored part of the forever lost identity of this ancient deity.
